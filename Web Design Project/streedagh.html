Streedagh Beach

About Streedagh Beach

Streedagh Beach is a stunning three-kilometre stretch of wild Atlantic coastline in north County Sligo. Backed by sand dunes and with views of Benbulben to the east and Inishmurray Island to the west, it is one of the most dramatic and unspoilt beaches in Ireland.

The beach is famous for its connection to the Spanish Armada of 1588. Three Spanish ships were wrecked at Streedagh Point during a violent Atlantic storm, and over 1,100 men were reported lost on the strand. One survivor, Captain Francisco de Cuéllar, escaped and wrote a remarkable account of his time in Ireland that survives to this day.

The beach also contains fossilised marine life embedded in the rocks along the shoreline, left over from the shallow tropical sea that covered this region 350 million years ago. Stone Age monuments including the remains of a court tomb can be found in the dunes at the north end.

What to See and Do

Streedagh is a Blue Flag beach popular with swimmers, surfers and fossil hunters. Walking the full length of the strand and exploring the dunes takes one to two hours. At low tide, look for fossil corals and shells in the flat rocks at the water's edge.

Good to Know

  • Beach length: 3km
  • Blue Flag status: Yes
  • Nearest town: Grange (2km)
  • Facilities: Car park, dog friendly

Getting There

From Sligo town, take the N15 north towards Grange. Follow the signs for Streedagh from Grange village. The beach car park is at the end of a narrow road through the dunes.
