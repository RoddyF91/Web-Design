About North Sligo

North Sligo is one of Ireland's most spectacular and least-visited regions, stretching along the Wild Atlantic Way between Sligo town and the Donegal border. The landscape is dominated by the flat-topped limestone plateau of Benbulben and the sweeping Atlantic coastline.

The area is steeped in history spanning thousands of years. From the Neolithic court tomb at Creevykeel, one of the oldest and best-preserved monuments in Ireland, to the wreck sites of three Spanish Armada ships at Streedagh Beach, North Sligo is a place where history is visible at every turn.

W.B. Yeats, Ireland's most celebrated poet and Nobel Laureate, drew inspiration from this landscape throughout his life. He is buried at Drumcliff Churchyard in the shadow of Benbulben, and his grave remains one of the most visited literary sites in Ireland.

The Gleniff Horseshoe, a glacial valley encircled by towering limestone cliffs, offers some of the most dramatic scenery on the island. The valley is associated with the ancient legend of Diarmuid and Gráinne and contains a famous cave believed to have sheltered the mythological lovers.

North Sligo in Focus

Three perspectives on what makes this corner of Ireland so remarkable.

A Landscape Shaped by Ice

The dramatic scenery of North Sligo was sculpted over millions of years. The limestone plateaux of Benbulben and the Dartry Mountains were formed when this region lay beneath a warm tropical sea 320 million years ago. Layers of marine sediment compressed into the rock visible in the cliffs today. The distinctive flat-topped shape of Benbulben was then carved by glaciers during the last Ice Age, which ended around 10,000 years ago.

History and Heritage

North Sligo has been inhabited for thousands of years. Neolithic farming communities built elaborate stone tombs here over 5,000 years ago, many of which still stand today. The Spanish Armada added a dramatic chapter in 1588, when three ships were wrecked at Streedagh during a fierce Atlantic storm. In the 19th century, Lord Palmerston built Classiebawn Castle and the stone harbour at Mullaghmore, leaving a lasting mark on the landscape.

Yeats Country

William Butler Yeats described Sligo as the land of heart's desire. Born in Dublin in 1865, Yeats spent much of his childhood in Sligo and returned throughout his life, drawing on its mythology and landscapes for his poetry. He was awarded the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1923. Yeats died in France in 1939 and was reinterred at Drumcliff Churchyard, beneath Benbulben, in 1948. His epitaph reads: Cast a cold eye on life, on death. Horseman, pass by.
